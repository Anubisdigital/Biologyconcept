Interesting Science Facts 1.0

​Fundamental Unit of Life:


All known living organisms are made up of one or more cells.

Some organisms, like bacteria and yeast, are unicellular (single-celled), while others, like plants and animals, are multicellular (many-celled).

​The Cell Theory: This is a cornerstone of biology, stating that:

​All living organisms are composed of cells.

​The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.

​All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

​Genetic Material:

All cells contain genetic information in the form of DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid), which serves as a blueprint for the cell's functions and is passed down to new cells during division.

​Types of Cells

​There are two main categories of cells:

Prokaryotic Cells: These are the simplest and oldest types of cells.

They lack a true nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Their genetic material floats freely in the cytoplasm. Bacteria and archaea are examples of prokaryotic organisms.
Eukaryotic Cells: These are more complex and larger.

They have a membrane-bound nucleus that contains the DNA and other specialized structures called organelles that perform specific tasks.

Animals, plants, fungi, and protists are made of eukaryotic cells.

Specialized Human Cells ​The human body contains trillions of cells, with over 200 different types, each with a specialized function.

​Red blood cells transport oxygen throughout the body.

​Neurons (nerve cells) transmit electrical and chemical signals to communicate with the brain.

​Muscle cells are responsible for movement and are organized into tissues like skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle.

​Skin cells (epithelial cells) form a protective barrier against the external environment.

​Some cells, like the ovum (egg cell), are among the largest human cells, visible to the naked eye.

In contrast, sperm cells are the smallest.

The eye is made up of several key parts, each with a specific function:
Cornea:
This is the clear, dome-shaped outer layer at the front of the eye.

It helps to focus light as it enters the eye.

Iris: The iris is the colored part of the eye.

It's a muscle that controls the size of the pupil, regulating how much light enters the eye.

Pupil:
 This is the black circle in the center of the iris. It's actually an opening that allows light to pass through.

Lens:
Located behind the iris and pupil, the lens is a transparent structure that fine-tunes the focusing of light onto the retina.

It changes shape to focus on objects at different distances.
Retina:
This is a light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye.

It contains millions of photoreceptor cells, including rods (for low-light vision) and cones (for color vision).

The retina converts light into electrical signals.

Optic Nerve:
This nerve connects the retina to the brain.

It transmits the electrical signals from the retina to the visual cortex in the brain, where the signals are processed and interpreted as the images we see.

How We See Seeing is a process that involves both the eye and the brain.

It begins when light rays enter the eye and are focused by the cornea and lens onto the retina.

The photoreceptor cells in the retina then convert this light into electrical signals.

These signals travel along the optic nerve to the brain. The brain's visual cortex receives these signals and flips the image (since the lens projects an inverted image) and combines the information from both eyes to create a single, three-dimensional image.

The Process of Blood Clotting: When blood is exposed to air as a result of a cut or wound, the platelets in the blood at the damaged tissue stimulate the release of a chemical called thromboplastin (thrombokinase). In the presence of calcium ions and vitamin K, thromboplastin stimulates the conversion of prothrombin to thrombin enzyme.

Thrombin then catalyzes the conversion of soluble blood protein fibrinogen to the insoluble form fibrin.

Fibrin forms fibers, which form a mesh and trap blood cells and proteins.

This mesh dries to form a scab, which is called the blood clot.

CONTROL OF BODY TEMPERATURE (Temperature regulation).



This is the process of maintaining the temperature of the organism within narrow ranges, which favour body activity, and ensures optimum activity of body enzymes.

To maintain the body temperature constant, there must be a balance between heat loss and heat gain. The body loses heat by;

a) Radiation: Heat diffuses from the warm body to the cold environment.

b) Conduction: The body loses heat to the cold object in contact with it.

c) Convection: Where cold air or wind carries heat from the warm body.

d) Evaporation: e.g. sweating leading to loss of heat

The body gains heat by;

a) Radiation: e.g. from the sun’s heat and reflection from the ground.

b) Conduction: e.g. from the ground via the feet.

c) Convection: e.g. from the wind bringing hot air to the body.

d) Metabolism:
e.g. since many of the body’s chemical reactions release heat e.g. in respiration.
The rate of heat loss and gain depends on; a) Surface area to volume ratio i.e.
Small organisms having a large surface area to volume ration tend to lose more heat than the large ones with small surface area to volume ratio.

b) Temperature of surrounding environment: Organisms tend to lose more heat in cold environment and gain more in hot environment.
c) Rate of respiration

The higher the rate of respiration, the more heat energy gained by the body.

d) Humidity of the environment Heat loss increases in humid conditions because high humidity makes the environment colder.

Endothermic/Homoithermic animals:

Endothermic organisms are those that are able to maintain a constant body temperature irrespective of the surrounding environmental temperature.

They depend mainly on heat generated within their bodies.

They are also called warm blooded animals e.g. mammals.

Ectothermic/poiklothermic animals:
These are animals that cannot maintain a constant body temperature but their temperature changes with that of the environment.

They are also called cold blooded animals e.g. reptiles and amphibians.

The digestive system, a series of hollow organs joined in a long, twisting tube from the mouth to the anus, processes the food you eat.

The respiratory system is a fascinating and vital biological system that takes in oxygen and expels carbon dioxide.

 ​The upper respiratory tract includes the parts that are outside the chest cavity.

​The lower respiratory tract consists of the organs within the chest cavity, where gas exchange occurs.

A tube that connects the larynx to the bronchi.

Its walls are supported by rings of cartilage to keep the airway open.

​Bronchi and Bronchioles:
The trachea branches into two main bronchi, one leading to each lung.

These tubes divide into smaller and smaller branches called bronchioles, which deliver air to the tiny air sacs.

​Lungs:

The main organs of the respiratory system. The right lung has three lobes, while the left lung has two, leaving space for the heart.

​Alveoli:
These are tiny, balloon-like air sacs at the ends of the bronchioles.

The walls of the alveoli are very thin and are surrounded by a network of tiny blood vessels called capillaries. This is where gas exchange takes place:
oxygen diffuses from the alveol i into the blood, and carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the alveoli to be exhaled.

​Structure and Function




​The alveoli are located at the end of the bronchioles, the smallest branches of the respiratory tree.

There are hundreds of millions of them in the human lungs, giving the lungs a huge surface area for gas exchange.

The walls of the alveoli are extremely thin, allowing gases to pass through easily. They are surrounded by a dense network of tiny blood vessels called capillaries. ​Diaphragm:
A large, dome-shaped muscle located below the lungs.

It is the main muscle of respiration. When you inhale, the diaphragm contracts and flattens, increasing the volume of the chest cavity and pulling air into the lungs.


When you exhale, the diaphragm relaxes, and the chest cavity decreases in size, pushing air out.
​The Breathing Process
​Breathing, or respiration, is a two-step process: ​Inhalation (Inspiration):
The diaphragm contracts, and the intercostal muscles between the ribs also contract, causing the chest cavity to expand.

This creates a vacuum, pulling air into the lungs.

​Exhalation (Expiration):

The diaphragm and intercostal muscles relax, reducing the size of the chest cavity.

This pushes air out of the lungs, expelling carbon dioxide.
​How it Works with Other Systems
 ​The respiratory system doesn't work in isolation. It is closely connected to other body systems, especially the circulatory system.

The circulatory system acts as the body's transport network. It picks up oxygen from the lungs and delivers it to every cell in the body.

At the same time, it picks up carbon dioxide, a waste product of metabolism, from the cells and transports it back to the lungs to be exhaled.

Classification of Living Things


The classification of living things is a core part of the science of biology. It's also known as taxonomy, and it's the practice of grouping organisms together based on their shared characteristics. This helps scientists to organize and understand the immense diversity of life on Earth. The main goal is to create a universal system that reflects the evolutionary relationships between different organisms.


The most widely used system today is the Linnaean taxonomy, developed by the Swedish botanist Carolus Linnaeus in the 18th century. His system uses a hierarchical structure, moving from very broad categories to much more specific ones. This structure is a fundamental concept in biological studies.


The Ranks of Classification


The Linnaean system uses a series of ranks, each one more specific than the last. Let's break down the main ranks, starting from the broadest:


Domain: This is the highest rank. There are three domains that encompass all life on Earth: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. Eukarya includes all organisms with a nucleus, such as plants, animals, fungi, and protists.


Kingdom: The next level down. Traditionally, living things were sorted into five kingdoms: Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia. However, modern science has revised this, often using a six-kingdom model or more complex structures based on genetic evidence. The most common of these include Animalia, Plantae, and Fungi.


Phylum: A significant division within a kingdom. For instance, within the kingdom Animalia, the phylum Chordata includes all animals with a backbone, while the phylum Arthropoda includes insects, spiders, and crustaceans.


Class: A group of related orders. The phylum Chordata includes classes such as Mammalia (mammals), Reptilia (reptiles), and Aves (birds).


Order: A group of related families. The class Mammalia includes orders like Primates (monkeys, apes, and humans) and Cetacea (whales and dolphins).


Family: A group of related genera. The order Primates includes the family Hominidae (great apes and humans) and Cercopithecidae (Old World monkeys).


Genus: A group of very closely related species. The family Hominidae contains the genus Homo (humans) and the genus Pan (chimpanzees).


Species: This is the most specific rank. A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. Our species is Homo sapiens. This is the fundamental unit of classification.


Binomial Nomenclature


Linnaeus also developed the system of binomial nomenclature, which gives every organism a unique two-part scientific name. The name is composed of the genus and the species, and it is always written in italics. The genus name is capitalized, while the species name is lowercase. For example, the scientific name for a domestic dog is Canis lupus. This system provides a universal and unambiguous way to refer to any specific organism, avoiding the confusion of common names that vary by language and region.


Why is Classification Important?


Classification is not just about organizing information; it provides valuable insights into evolution and biological relationships. It helps scientists to:


  • Provide a universal language for biologists worldwide.
  • Understand evolutionary relationships by showing how different groups of organisms are related to each other.
  • Predict the characteristics of newly discovered organisms based on their classification.
  • Help manage and conserve biodiversity by providing a framework for studying and protecting different species.

The field of taxonomy is constantly evolving with new discoveries and advancements in genetic sequencing. This ongoing process refines our understanding of the vast and intricate tree of life on our planet.


The Deeper Science

The classification of living things, or taxonomy, is a vast and ever-evolving field of science. It is the practice and science of categorizing all organisms. The goal is not simply to create a list, but to build a system that reflects the evolutionary relationships and common ancestry between different forms of life. This is an exciting part of science because it's constantly being updated with new discoveries, particularly from genetics.


The Linnaean Hierarchy in Greater Detail

The system proposed by Carolus Linnaeus is the bedrock of modern taxonomy. His hierarchical structure is a set of nested categories, where each level gets more specific than the one above it. Let’s examine each of these ranks more closely, using the example of a familiar animal, the gray wolf (Canis lupus).


Domain: The highest rank, a very broad category. All life is divided into three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. The gray wolf is in the Eukarya domain because its cells have a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles.


Kingdom: The next level. Eukarya is split into four main kingdoms: Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, and Protista. The gray wolf is in the Animalia kingdom, as it is a multicellular, heterotrophic organism that consumes other organisms for energy.


Phylum: A major division within a kingdom. The kingdom Animalia has many phyla, including Chordata. The gray wolf is in the phylum Chordata, which includes all vertebrates, meaning they have a backbone.


Class: The phylum Chordata is divided into classes like Mammalia, Aves (birds), and Reptilia (reptiles). The gray wolf is in the class Mammalia because it is warm-blooded, has hair, and produces milk to feed its young.


Order: This groups related families. The class Mammalia includes orders like Primates (monkeys, humans) and Rodentia (rodents). The gray wolf belongs to the order Carnivora, which includes all meat-eating mammals.


Family: The order Carnivora includes families such as Felidae (cats) and Canidae (dogs, wolves, foxes). The gray wolf is in the family Canidae, a group of animals that are all descendants of a common ancestor.


Genus: This is a group of very closely related species. The family Canidae includes the genus Canis, which contains wolves, dogs, coyotes, and jackals. These animals are so closely related that they share very recent common ancestry.


Species: The most specific rank. Organisms of the same species can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. The gray wolf's species is lupus. The full scientific name is Canis lupus.


Beyond Linnaeus: The Rise of Modern Phylogenetics

While the Linnaean system is still used today, modern science has moved beyond it in some ways. The field of phylogenetics is the study of evolutionary relationships among groups of organisms. Instead of just grouping by physical traits, scientists now use DNA sequencing, RNA sequencing, and molecular data to build evolutionary trees, also known as cladograms. These trees show the exact lineage and common ancestors of different species. This has led to major revisions in the tree of life, as some organisms that looked similar were found to be genetically very different, and vice-versa.


A key concept in phylogenetics is the clade, which is a group of organisms that includes a common ancestor and all of its descendants. For example, the great apes (chimpanzees, gorillas, orangutans) and humans form a clade because they all share a common ancestor. This modern approach is more accurate and provides a truer picture of life’s history on Earth.


The Importance of a Robust Classification System

Classification is much more than a simple organizational tool. It's essential for several reasons:


1. Universal Communication: A scientific name like Panthera leo for a lion is understood by scientists everywhere, regardless of their native language. This prevents confusion with common names that can be different in various parts of the world.


2. Understanding Evolution: By seeing which organisms are grouped together, we can infer their evolutionary relationships. This helps us trace the history of life and understand how different traits evolved over millions of years.


3. Conservation and Biodiversity: A clear classification system helps us identify and catalog the planet's vast biodiversity. This is crucial for protecting endangered species and understanding ecosystems. Knowing a species' family and genus can give clues about its biology, which can inform conservation efforts.


4. Medical and Agricultural Applications: Classifying organisms helps us identify pathogens and pests, which is critical for developing treatments and protecting food supplies. Knowing that two disease-causing bacteria belong to the same genus can help predict how they might behave or what treatments might be effective.


In summary, the classification of living things is a powerful and essential tool that reveals the interconnectedness of all life on our planet. It is a fundamental part of the scientific discipline of biology.



Genetics

Genetics and heredity are fundamental concepts in biology that explain how traits are passed from parents to offspring. Genetics is the scientific study of genes, genetic variation, and heredity. Heredity, on the other hand, is the process by which a parent passes certain genes to their children.


​The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology ​

The flow of genetic information in a cell is described by the Central Dogma of Molecular Biology. It explains how DNA is transcribed into RNA, and RNA is then translated into protein.
DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the genetic blueprint of an organism. It's a double-stranded helix that contains the instructions for making all the proteins in a cell. Genes are specific segments of DNA that contain these instructions.
Transcription is the process where a gene's DNA sequence is copied into a messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule. This happens in the nucleus of a cell. The mRNA then travels out of the nucleus to the cytoplasm.
Translation is the process where the mRNA sequence is used to create a protein. This occurs at the ribosomes. The sequence of nucleotides in the mRNA is read in groups of three, called codons, each of which corresponds to a specific amino acid. These amino acids are then linked together to form a protein.


key Concepts in Heredity

​

Alleles are different versions of the same gene. For example, a gene for eye color might have an allele for blue eyes and another for brown eyes.
An organism's genotype is its complete set of genes, while its phenotype is the observable traits resulting from those genes. For instance, the genotype might be the alleles for brown and blue eyes, but the phenotype is the actual brown eye color.
Dominant alleles are expressed even if only one copy is present, while recessive alleles are only expressed if two copies are present. In the eye color example, the allele for brown eyes is typically dominant over the allele for blue eyes.
A person with two identical alleles for a gene is homozygous, while a person with two different alleles is heterozygous. A person with one dominant and one recessive allele for eye color is heterozygous but will have the brown-eyed phenotype.


The Work of Gregor Mendel

​

The field of genetics was pioneered by Gregor Mendel, an Austrian monk who conducted experiments with pea plants in the mid-19th century. His work laid the foundation for modern genetics. He developed two key laws:
The Law of Segregation states that during the formation of gametes (sperm and egg cells), the two alleles for a trait separate so that each gamete only carries one allele.
The Law of Independent Assortment states that the alleles of two or more different genes are sorted into gametes independently of one another. This means that the inheritance of one trait does not influence the inheritance of another.


​Beyond Mendel: Modern Genetics ​

While Mendel's laws are fundamental, not all inheritance patterns follow them perfectly. Incomplete dominance occurs when a heterozygous individual shows a phenotype that is a blend of the two alleles. For example, a red flower and a white flower might produce pink offspring.
Codominance is when both alleles are expressed equally in the phenotype. A great example is human blood type, where both A and B alleles can be expressed at the same time, resulting in AB blood type.
Polygenic inheritance involves traits controlled by multiple genes, such as height and skin color. This results in a wide range of phenotypes, not just a few distinct categories.
Understanding these concepts helps us explain everything from why we have our parents' eye color to why we're susceptible to certain diseases. It's a vast and fascinating field.


An In-Depth Look

Genetics and heredity are the cornerstone of modern biology. Genetics is the scientific study of genes, heredity, and the variation of inherited characteristics in living organisms. Heredity is the process by which traits are passed from parents to their offspring. Together, they explain why offspring resemble their parents but are not identical to them. The entire field is built on the incredible discovery of the gene, the fundamental unit of heredity.


The Blueprint of Life: DNA, Genes, and Chromosomes

At the heart of genetics is Deoxyribonucleic acid, or DNA. DNA is a complex molecule that looks like a twisted ladder, known as the double helix. It contains all the instructions needed for an organism to develop, survive, and reproduce. A gene is a specific segment of this DNA that codes for a particular trait or protein. Genes are located on structures called chromosomes, which are long strands of DNA tightly coiled with proteins. Humans typically have 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs within each of their cells.


The Father of Modern Genetics: Gregor Mendel

Our understanding of heredity began with the work of an Austrian monk named Gregor Mendel in the 19th century. Through his meticulous experiments with pea plants, he established the basic principles of inheritance. He observed that traits like flower color or plant height were not blended in offspring but were inherited as discrete units, which we now call genes. Mendel formulated two key laws:


1. The Law of Segregation: This law states that an organism inherits one allele for each gene from each parent. During the formation of gametes (sperm and egg cells), these two alleles separate, so each gamete only carries one allele.


2. The Law of Independent Assortment: This law states that the inheritance of one gene is independent of the inheritance of another. For example, the gene for pea color is passed on independently of the gene for pea shape, as long as they are on separate chromosomes.


Key Terminology in Heredity

To understand how traits are passed on, it is essential to know these terms:


Allele: An allele is a specific version of a gene. For example, for the gene for flower color in pea plants, there can be a purple allele and a white allele.


Genotype: The genotype is the genetic makeup of an organism. It refers to the specific combination of alleles an organism has for a given trait.


Phenotype: The phenotype is the physical, observable trait that is expressed. It is the result of the genotype and environmental factors.


Dominant and Recessive Alleles: A dominant allele will always be expressed in the phenotype, even if only one copy is present. A recessive allele is only expressed when two copies of it are present (one from each parent).


Homozygous and Heterozygous: An organism is homozygous for a gene if it has two identical alleles (e.g., two dominant or two recessive). It is heterozygous if it has two different alleles (one dominant and one recessive).


How Traits are Inherited: Punnett Squares and Meiosis

A Punnett square is a simple diagram used to predict the possible genotypes of offspring from a genetic cross. It's a visual tool that demonstrates the principles of segregation and independent assortment. For example, crossing two heterozygous parents (both having one dominant and one recessive allele) for a trait would result in offspring with a predictable ratio of genotypes and phenotypes.


The biological process behind this is meiosis, the type of cell division that produces gametes (sperm and egg cells). During meiosis, the pairs of chromosomes are separated and randomly distributed to the gametes. This ensures that each gamete has only one set of chromosomes, allowing for the correct number of chromosomes to be restored when a sperm fertilizes an egg.


Beyond Mendel: Complex Inheritance Patterns

While Mendel's work provides a fundamental framework, not all traits follow a simple dominant/recessive pattern. Other inheritance patterns include:


Incomplete Dominance: This occurs when one allele is not completely dominant over the other, leading to a blended or intermediate phenotype. For example, a red flower crossed with a white flower might produce pink offspring.


Codominance: This is when both alleles are fully expressed in the phenotype. A classic example is human blood type, where a person with both A and B alleles has a blood type of AB, with both traits being visible.


Polygenic Inheritance: Many traits, such as human height and skin color, are controlled by multiple genes. This results in a wide range of phenotypes, not just a few distinct categories.


Genetics continues to be a frontier of science, with ongoing research into gene expression, genetic engineering, and personalized medicine. The study of how our DNA shapes who we are is perhaps one of the most profound and fascinating areas of biology.


Ecology and Interdependence

​

Ecology is the scientific study of the interactions between living organisms and their environment. It explores how organisms, from the smallest bacteria to the largest whales, are affected by and adapt to their surroundings. It's a vast field that covers everything from a single bacterium's role in the soil to the complex dynamics of a global rainforest.

​
​

Within ecology, one of the most fundamental concepts is interdependence. This refers to the mutual reliance of organisms on one another and on their physical environment for survival. No species exists in isolation. Every organism is part of a complex web of relationships that includes predator-prey dynamics, symbiotic relationships, and the cycling of nutrients.


​

Predator-Prey Relationships

​

Predator-prey relationships are a classic example of interdependence. Predators, like a lion, rely on their prey, like a zebra, for food. At the same time, the population of the prey species can influence the population of the predator. For example, if the zebra population declines due to disease, the lion population may also decline due to a lack of food. This constant fluctuation helps maintain a natural balance in the ecosystem.

​
​

Symbiotic Relationships

​

Symbiosis describes a close and long-term interaction between two different biological species. There are several types:


Mutualism: Both species benefit from the relationship. A well-known example is the relationship between bees and flowers. The bees get nectar for food, and the flowers are pollinated, allowing them to reproduce.


Commensalism: One species benefits, while the other is neither helped nor harmed. An example is a barnacle living on a whale. The barnacle gets a ride and a new habitat, while the whale is unaffected.


Parasitism: One species (the parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (the host). Ticks on a dog are a good example. The tick gets a blood meal, and the dog can suffer from irritation or disease.


​

All of these intricate connections highlight the fragile and interconnected nature of life on Earth. A change in one part of an ecosystem, such as the removal of a key species, can have a domino effect throughout the entire system, leading to unexpected and sometimes devastating consequences.

​
​

Food Chains and Trophic Levels

​

One of the most straightforward ways to visualize interdependence is through a food chain. It's a linear sequence showing the flow of energy from one organism to another. For example, a grass plant is eaten by a rabbit, which is then eaten by a fox. However, nature is rarely so simple. A food web is a more accurate representation, showing the multiple, interconnected food chains within an ecosystem.

​
​

Within these food webs, organisms occupy different trophic levels.

​
​

Producers are the foundation. These are organisms, like plants and algae, that produce their own food using energy from the sun through photosynthesis.

​
​

Consumers are organisms that eat other organisms to get energy. These are further categorized: primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers; secondary consumers (carnivores or omnivores) eat primary consumers; and tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers.

​
​

The Critical Role of Decomposers

​

Often overlooked, decomposers, such as bacteria and fungi, are essential to interdependence. They break down dead organic matter and waste products, returning vital nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus back into the soil. Without decomposers, the nutrients would be locked away in dead organisms, and the ecosystem would eventually run out of the building blocks for life.

​
​

Keystone Species

​

Some species have a disproportionately large effect on their environment relative to their abundance. These are called keystone species. The removal of a keystone species can cause a dramatic shift or even the collapse of an entire ecosystem. For instance, the reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone National Park helped to control the elk population, which in turn allowed for the regrowth of trees and the return of beavers and other animals.

​
​

Human Impact on Interdependence

​

Human activities have a significant impact on these intricate relationships. Habitat destruction, pollution, and the introduction of non-native species can sever vital connections, leading to a ripple effect throughout the ecosystem. For example, deforestation not only removes a habitat but also disrupts the delicate balance of a food web, affecting all the species that relied on that forest for food and shelter. Understanding interdependence is crucial to conserving our planet's biodiversity and ensuring the long-term health of our ecosystems.
